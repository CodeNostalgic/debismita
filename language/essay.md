### Screens Are Killing Children's Imagination

In today's digital world, screens have become an inseparable part of children's lives. From online classes and educational videos to games and social media, children spend several hours every day staring at phones, tablets, computers, or televisions. While technology has undoubtedly made learning easier and more accessible, I strongly believe that excessive screen time is slowly diminishing children's imagination and creativity.

Imagination flourishes when children explore, observe, question, and create. In the past, children spent their evenings reading storybooks, building toy houses, drawing pictures, inventing games, or simply playing outdoors with friends. A cardboard box could become a spaceship, and a stick could transform into a magical sword. Such activities encouraged them to think independently and develop original ideas.

Today, however, screens provide ready-made images, stories, and solutions. Instead of imagining how a dragon might look, children merely watch animated dragons on a screen. Video games and short videos entertain them instantly, leaving little room for creative thinking. Since every scene is presented visually, the brain has fewer opportunities to construct its own images or invent new possibilities.

Moreover, excessive screen time often reduces meaningful social interaction. Children who spend hours on digital devices may miss conversations with family members, outdoor adventures, and group activities that teach cooperation, empathy, and problem-solving. These real-life experiences play a vital role in shaping a child's imagination.

This does not mean that technology is harmful in itself. Educational apps, documentaries, coding platforms, and digital art tools can inspire creativity when used wisely. The real problem arises when entertainment dominates learning and screen time replaces books, hobbies, sports, and imaginative play.

In conclusion, screens are wonderful servants but poor masters. Parents and teachers must encourage children to strike a healthy balance between technology and traditional activities. A child's imagination is like a young plant—it grows best when it receives the sunshine of curiosity, the water of experience, and the freedom to dream beyond the limits of a screen.


## Grades Do Not Define a Student's Intelligence

In almost every school, examination results are eagerly awaited by students, parents, and teachers. High grades are often celebrated as a sign of intelligence, while poor marks are viewed as failure. However, I strongly disagree with the statement that grades define a student's intelligence. Although grades measure academic performance to some extent, they cannot fully reflect a person's abilities, creativity, or potential.

Firstly, intelligence is a broad concept. Some students excel in mathematics and science, while others possess remarkable talent in music, art, sports, writing, or public speaking. A student who struggles with algebra may compose beautiful poems or play a musical instrument with extraordinary skill. Such talents cannot be measured through written examinations alone.

Secondly, examinations test performance on a particular day under specific conditions. A student may perform poorly due to illness, stress, anxiety, or personal problems despite having studied sincerely. Similarly, some students are excellent at memorising facts and scoring high marks but may find it difficult to apply their knowledge in real-life situations. Therefore, grades reveal only a part of a student's capabilities.

History provides numerous examples of successful people who were not outstanding in school. Many inventors, entrepreneurs, artists, and athletes achieved greatness through perseverance, creativity, and determination rather than exceptional report cards. Their achievements remind us that intelligence is not confined to examination halls.

This does not mean that grades are unimportant. They encourage discipline, regular study habits, and healthy competition. Good grades also open doors to higher education and scholarships. Nevertheless, they should be regarded as indicators of academic progress rather than the sole measure of intelligence.

In conclusion, every child is unique and possesses different strengths. True education should nurture curiosity, critical thinking, kindness, creativity, and resilience alongside academic excellence. Grades may reflect how well a student performs in examinations, but they can never define the depth of a person's intelligence or determine the success that awaits them in life.

## Technological Evolution Has Made Students Lazy Thinkers

The rapid advancement of technology has transformed the way students learn. The internet, artificial intelligence, educational apps, and digital libraries have placed an ocean of knowledge at our fingertips. While these innovations have made education more accessible, I believe that excessive dependence on technology is gradually making many students lazy thinkers.

Earlier, students visited libraries, referred to several books, and spent time analysing information before arriving at a conclusion. Today, a simple search engine or an AI tool can provide instant answers within seconds. Although this saves time, it often discourages students from questioning, analysing, and thinking independently. Many simply copy information without understanding the concepts behind it.

Another concern is the decline in problem-solving skills. Whether it is solving mathematical equations, writing essays, or completing projects, students often rely on calculators, online solutions, or AI-generated responses. As a result, they may finish their work quickly but fail to develop logical reasoning and creativity. The habit of finding instant answers weakens the ability to think deeply and solve unfamiliar problems.

Technology has also reduced the attention span of many students. Short videos, instant notifications, and endless scrolling encourage the brain to seek quick entertainment rather than sustained concentration. Reading a lengthy chapter or solving a challenging problem now seems difficult for many learners because they have become accustomed to instant gratification.

However, technology itself is not the enemy. When used wisely, it can inspire curiosity and innovation. Virtual laboratories, educational videos, coding platforms, and online courses have enabled millions of students to learn beyond the boundaries of the classroom. The real issue lies in how technology is used. It should assist thinking, not replace it.

In conclusion, technological evolution has brought remarkable opportunities, but students must avoid becoming dependent on it for every answer. True education develops the ability to question, reason, imagine, and create. Technology should be a powerful tool in the hands of thoughtful learners, not a shortcut that weakens the habit of independent thinking. A balanced approach will ensure that students remain both technologically skilled and intellectually strong.
