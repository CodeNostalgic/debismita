### Synopsis (before the scene begins)
**Antonio, a Venetian merchant, has invested all his wealth in trading expeditions. Bassanio, his friend and kinsman, asks him for money to go to Belmont, where Bassanio hopes to marry the heiress Portia. Antonio gives Bassanio permission to borrow the money on Antonio’s credit.**

→ This is a modern summary. It sets up the central friendship, Antonio’s generosity/risk-taking, and the plot engine (Bassanio’s courtship of Portia).

---

**Enter Antonio, Salarino, and Solanio.**

**ANTONIO**  
**In sooth I know not why I am so sad.**  
“In truth, I don’t know why I’m so sad.”  
(“In sooth” = truly/honestly)

**It wearies me, you say it wearies you.**  
It tires me out, and you say it tires you too (to see me like this).

**But how I caught it, found it, or came by it,**  
But how I got this sadness, where it came from,

**What stuff ’tis made of, whereof it is born,**  
What it’s composed of, or how it originated,

**I am to learn.**  
I still have to figure out. (He has no idea why he feels melancholy.)

**And such a want-wit sadness makes of me**  
And this sadness turns me into such a fool (“want-wit” = lacking wit/intelligence)

**That I have much ado to know myself.**  
That I can barely even recognize or understand my own feelings.

---

**SALARINO**  
**Your mind is tossing on the ocean,**  
Your thoughts are out at sea, worrying about your ships.

**There where your argosies with portly sail**  
Where your large merchant ships (“argosies” = big trading vessels) with their full, dignified sails

**(Like signiors and rich burghers on the flood,**  
(They look like important gentlemen and wealthy citizens on the water,)

**Or, as it were, the pageants of the sea)**  
Or like grand floating spectacles/processions of the sea)

**Do overpeer the petty traffickers**  
Tower over the small trading boats

**That curtsy to them, do them reverence,**  
Which bow to them and show respect,

**As they fly by them with their woven wings.**  
As your big ships sail past them swiftly with their canvas sails (“woven wings”).

→ Salarino assumes Antonio’s sadness is caused by anxiety over his risky sea ventures.

---

**SOLANIO**  
**Believe me, sir, had I such venture forth,**  
Believe me, if I had such valuable cargo at sea,

**The better part of my affections would**  
Most of my emotions/heart

**Be with my hopes abroad.**  
Would be with my hopes overseas.

**I should be still**  
I would constantly be

**Plucking the grass to know where sits the wind,**  
Pulling up grass to test wind direction (a common sailor’s trick),

**Piring in maps for ports and piers and roads;**  
Staring closely at maps for safe harbors, piers, and shipping routes;

**And every object that might make me fear**  
And any sight that might suggest

**Misfortune to my ventures, out of doubt**  
Danger to my investments, without doubt

**Would make me sad.**  
Would make me sad.

---

**SALARINO**  
**My wind cooling my broth**  
The wind that cools my soup

**Would blow me to an ague when I thought**  
Would make me shiver with fever (ague = chills) whenever I thought

**What harm a wind too great might do at sea.**  
What damage a strong gale could do to ships at sea.

**I should not see the sandy hourglass run**  
I wouldn’t watch sand running in an hourglass

**But I should think of shallows and of flats,**  
Without thinking of shallow waters and sandbanks,

**And see my wealthy Andrew docked in sand,**  
And imagine my rich ship (named *Andrew*) stuck in sand,

**Vailing her high top lower than her ribs**  
Lowering her tall mast (top) below her sides

**To kiss her burial.**  
As if kissing its own grave (sinking).

**Should I go to church**  
If I went to church

**And see the holy edifice of stone**  
And saw the stone church building

**And not bethink me straight of dangerous rocks,**  
I would immediately think of dangerous rocks (that could wreck ships),

**Which, touching but my gentle vessel’s side,**  
Which, just by touching my smooth ship’s side,

**Would scatter all her spices on the stream,**  
Would spill all the valuable spices into the sea,

**Enrobe the roaring waters with my silks,**  
Cover the waves with my expensive silk cargo,

**And, in a word, but even now worth this**  
And, in short, something that moments ago was worth a fortune

**And now worth nothing?**  
Now worth nothing?

**Shall I have the thought**  
Should I have the thought

**To think on this, and shall I lack the thought**  
Of thinking about this, and yet not realize

**That such a thing bechanced would make me sad?**  
That if such a disaster happened, it would make me sad?

**But tell not me: I know Antonio**  
Don’t argue with me: I know Antonio

**Is sad to think upon his merchandise.**  
Is sad because he’s worrying about his goods/cargo.

---

**ANTONIO**  
**Believe me, no. I thank my fortune for it,**  
Believe me, no. I’m grateful to fortune that

**My ventures are not in one bottom trusted,**  
My investments are not all in one ship (“bottom” = ship’s hull),

**Nor to one place; nor is my whole estate**  
Nor sent to one single place; nor is my entire fortune

**Upon the fortune of this present year:**  
Dependent on this year’s success.

**Therefore my merchandise makes me not sad.**  
So my business ventures aren’t making me sad.

---

**SOLANIO**  
**Why then you are in love.**  
Then you must be in love.

**ANTONIO**  
**Fie, fie!**  
Nonsense! (Expression of disgust/annoyance)

**SOLANIO**  
**Not in love neither? Then let us say you are sad**  
Not in love either? Then let’s just say you’re sad

**Because you are not merry; and ’twere as easy**  
Because you’re not happy; and it would be just as easy

**For you to laugh and leap, and say you are merry**  
For you to laugh and jump around and claim you’re merry

**Because you are not sad.**  
Simply because you’re not sad (circular logic).

**Now, by two-headed Janus,**  
Now, by Janus (Roman god with two faces, looking forward and backward),

**Nature hath framed strange fellows in her time:**  
Nature has created some odd people:

**Some that will evermore peep through their eyes**  
Some who always squint/look through half-closed eyes

**And laugh like parrots at a bagpiper,**  
And laugh foolishly at anything (even inappropriate things like a bagpiper),

**And other of such vinegar aspect**  
And others with such sour faces

**That they’ll not show their teeth in way of smile**  
Who won’t smile even slightly

**Though Nestor swear the jest be laughable.**  
Even if the wise old Nestor (from Homer) swore the joke was funny.

→ Solanio jokes about different personality types — overly cheerful vs. overly gloomy.

---

**Enter Bassanio, Lorenzo, and Gratiano.**

**Here comes Bassanio, your most noble kinsman,**  
Here comes Bassanio, your noble relative,

**Gratiano, and Lorenzo. Fare you well.**  
Gratiano, and Lorenzo. Goodbye.

**We leave you now with better company.**

**SALARINO**  
**I would have stayed till I had made you merry,**  
I would have stayed until I cheered you up,

**If worthier friends had not prevented me.**  
If more important friends hadn’t arrived.

**ANTONIO**  
**Your worth is very dear in my regard.**  
I value you highly.

**I take it your own business calls on you,**  
I assume your own affairs require you to leave,

**And you embrace th’ occasion to depart.**  
And you’re taking the opportunity to go.

**SALARINO**  
**Good morrow, my good lords.**  
Good morning, gentlemen.

**BASSANIO**  
**Good signiors both, when shall we laugh? Say, when?**  
Gentlemen, when shall we meet to enjoy ourselves? When?

**You grow exceeding strange. Must it be so?**  
You’ve become very distant lately. Does it have to be this way?

**SALARINO**  
**We’ll make our leisures to attend on yours.**  
We’ll make time to join you whenever you want.

*Salarino and Solanio exit.*

---

**LORENZO**  
**My Lord Bassanio, since you have found Antonio,**  
Lord Bassanio, now that you’ve found Antonio,

**We two will leave you. But at dinner time**  
We two will leave you. But at dinner time

**I pray you have in mind where we must meet.**  
Please remember where we’re supposed to meet.

**BASSANIO**  
**I will not fail you.**

**GRATIANO**  
**You look not well, Signior Antonio.**  
You don’t look well, Signior Antonio.

**You have too much respect upon the world.**  
You worry too much about worldly matters.

**They lose it that do buy it with much care.**  
People who buy success with too much anxiety often lose it anyway.

**Believe me, you are marvelously changed.**  
You’ve changed a lot (become gloomy).

---

**ANTONIO**  
**I hold the world but as the world, Gratiano,**  
I see the world simply as it is, Gratiano,

**A stage where every man must play a part,**  
A stage where every person must play a role,

**And mine a sad one.**  
And mine happens to be a sad one.

---

**GRATIANO**  
**Let me play the fool.**  
Let me play the fool (the cheerful jester).

**With mirth and laughter let old wrinkles come,**  
Let me grow old with laughter and joy (wrinkles from smiling),

**And let my liver rather heat with wine**  
And let my liver be warmed by wine (liver associated with passion)

**Than my heart cool with mortifying groans.**  
Rather than my heart grow cold from depressing sighs.

**Why should a man whose blood is warm within**  
Why should a lively, warm-blooded man

**Sit like his grandsire cut in alabaster?**  
Sit like a cold stone statue of his grandfather?

**Sleep when he wakes? And creep into the jaundice**  
Sleep while awake? And turn yellow with jaundice (from melancholy)

**By being peevish?**  
Just by being irritable?

**I tell thee what, Antonio**  
Let me tell you, Antonio

**(I love thee, and ’tis my love that speaks):**  
(I love you, and it’s out of love that I speak):

**There are a sort of men whose visages**  
There are some men whose faces

**Do cream and mantle like a standing pond**  
Become covered with scum like stagnant water

**And do a willful stillness entertain**  
And deliberately stay silent and still

**With purpose to be dressed in an opinion**  
So they will be thought of as

**Of wisdom, gravity, profound conceit,**  
Wise, serious, and deeply intelligent,

**As who should say “I am Sir Oracle,**  
As if to say “I am Sir Oracle (all-knowing),

**And when I ope my lips, let no dog bark.”**  
And when I open my mouth, everyone must be silent.”

**O my Antonio, I do know of these**  
Oh Antonio, I know such people

**That therefore only are reputed wise**  
Who are considered wise

**For saying nothing,**  
Only because they say nothing,

**when, I am very sure,**  
when I’m sure that

**If they should speak, would almost damn those ears**  
If they spoke, they would annoy listeners so much

**Which, hearing them, would call their brothers fools.**  
That the listeners would think even wise people are fools.

**I’ll tell thee more of this another time.**  
I’ll say more about this later.

**But fish not with this melancholy bait**  
But don’t use your melancholy as bait

**For this fool gudgeon, this opinion.**  
To catch the worthless opinion of being thought wise. (“Gudgeon” = a small, easily caught fish, metaphor for gullibility)

**Come, good Lorenzo.—Fare you well a while.**  
Come, Lorenzo. Goodbye for now.

**I’ll end my exhortation after dinner.**  
I’ll finish my lecture after dinner.

---

**LORENZO**  
**Well, we will leave you then till dinner time.**  
We’ll leave you until dinner.

**I must be one of these same dumb wise men,**  
I must be one of those silent wise men,

**For Gratiano never lets me speak.**  
Because Gratiano never gives me a chance to talk.

**GRATIANO**  
**Well, keep me company but two years more,**  
If you stay with me two more years,

**Thou shalt not know the sound of thine own tongue.**  
You won’t even recognize the sound of your own voice (because I talk so much).

**ANTONIO**  
**Fare you well. I’ll grow a talker for this gear.**  
Goodbye. I’ll become more talkative because of this.

**GRATIANO**  
**Thanks, i’ faith, for silence is only commendable**  
Thanks, truly, because silence is only praiseworthy

**In a neat’s tongue dried and a maid not vendible.**  
In dried ox tongue (food) and an unattractive unmarried girl (“not vendible” = not marriageable).

*Gratiano and Lorenzo exit.*

---

**ANTONIO**  
**Is that anything now?**  
Was that worth anything? (referring to Gratiano’s long speech)

**BASSANIO**  
**Gratiano speaks an infinite deal of nothing,**  
Gratiano talks endlessly about nothing,

**more than any man in all Venice.**  
More than anyone else in Venice.

**His reasons are as two grains of wheat hid in two bushels of chaff:**  
His meaningful points are like two grains of wheat hidden in two bushels of worthless husks (chaff).

**you shall seek all day ere you find them, and when you have them, they are not worth the search.**  
You could search all day to find them, and once found, they’re not worth the effort.

---

**ANTONIO**  
**Well, tell me now what lady is the same**  
Well, tell me now — who is this lady

**To whom you swore a secret pilgrimage,**  
To whom you swore a secret journey/pilgrimage (romantic quest),

**That you today promised to tell me of?**  
That you promised today to tell me about?

---

**BASSANIO**  
**’Tis not unknown to you, Antonio,**  
You already know, Antonio,

**How much I have disabled mine estate**  
How much I have ruined my financial situation

**By something showing a more swelling port**  
By living in a more extravagant style

**Than my faint means would grant continuance.**  
Than my limited money could actually support.

**Nor do I now make moan to be abridged**  
I’m not complaining about having to cut back

**From such a noble rate.**  
From that luxurious lifestyle.

**But my chief care**  
But my main concern

**Is to come fairly off from the great debts**  
Is to honorably get clear of the large debts

**Wherein my time, something too prodigal,**  
That my rather wasteful youth

**Hath left me gaged.**  
Has left me owing.

**To you, Antonio,**  
To you, Antonio,

**I owe the most in money and in love,**  
I owe the most, both in money and in friendship/love,

**And from your love I have a warranty**  
And because of your love for me, I feel I have permission

**To unburden all my plots and purposes**  
To share all my plans and intentions

**How to get clear of all the debts I owe.**  
About how to clear my debts.

---

**ANTONIO**  
**I pray you, good Bassanio, let me know it;**  
Please tell me, good Bassanio;

**And if it stand, as you yourself still do,**  
And if it is honorable (as you yourself always are),

**Within the eye of honor, be assured**  
Within the bounds of honor, then be assured

**My purse, my person, my extremest means**  
My money, my body (effort), and everything I have

**Lie all unlocked to your occasions.**  
Are completely available for your needs.

---

**BASSANIO**  
**In my school days, when I had lost one shaft,**  
When I was a schoolboy and lost one arrow,

**I shot his fellow of the selfsame flight**  
I shot another arrow exactly the same way

**The selfsame way with more advisèd watch**  
With more careful attention

**To find the other forth; and by adventuring both**  
To recover the first one; and by risking both

**I oft found both.**  
I often found both.

**I urge this childhood proof**  
I mention this childhood example

**Because what follows is pure innocence.**  
Because what I’m about to ask is innocent and straightforward.

**I owe you much, and, like a willful youth,**  
I owe you a lot, and like a reckless young man,

**That which I owe is lost.**  
What I borrowed is gone (spent).

**But if you please**  
But if you’re willing

**To shoot another arrow that self way**  
To lend me more money in the same direction (for the same purpose),

**Which you did shoot the first, I do not doubt,**  
As you lent the first time, I don’t doubt

**As I will watch the aim, or to find both**  
That if I aim carefully, I will either recover both

**Or bring your latter hazard back again,**  
Or at least return the new loan to you,

**And thankfully rest debtor for the first.**  
And remain gratefully in your debt for the original amount.

---

**ANTONIO**  
**You know me well, and herein spend but time**  
You know me well, so you’re just wasting time

**To wind about my love with circumstance;**  
By approaching my friendship so indirectly;

**And out of doubt you do me now more wrong**  
And without doubt you wrong me more

**In making question of my uttermost**  
By doubting my complete willingness

**Than if you had made waste of all I have.**  
Than if you had actually wasted everything I own.

**Then do but say to me what I should do**  
Just tell me what I should do

**That in your knowledge may by me be done,**  
That you know I can do,

**And I am prest unto it. Therefore speak.**  
And I am ready for it. So speak.

---

**BASSANIO**  
**In Belmont is a lady richly left,**  
In Belmont there is a wealthy heiress (her father recently died),

**And she is fair, and, fairer than that word,**  
She is beautiful, and even more beautiful than the word suggests,

**Of wondrous virtues.**  
And possesses wonderful qualities.

**Sometimes from her eyes**  
Sometimes through her eyes

**I did receive fair speechless messages.**  
I received silent but clear messages of affection.

**Her name is Portia, nothing undervalued**  
Her name is Portia; she is in no way inferior

**To Cato’s daughter, Brutus’ Portia.**  
To Portia, the daughter of Cato and wife of Brutus (a famous Roman heroine known for virtue and bravery).

**Nor is the wide world ignorant of her worth,**  
The whole world knows her value,

**For the four winds blow in from every coast**  
Because suitors come from every direction

**Renownèd suitors, and her sunny locks**  
Famous suitors, and her golden hair

**Hang on her temples like a golden fleece,**  
Hangs like the mythical Golden Fleece,

**Which makes her seat of Belmont Colchos’ strond,**  
Making her home in Belmont like Colchis (where Jason sought the fleece),

**And many Jasons come in quest of her.**  
And many “Jasons” (adventurers) come seeking her.

**O my Antonio, had I but the means**  
Oh Antonio, if only I had the money

**To hold a rival place with one of them,**  
To compete on equal terms with one of them,

**I have a mind presages me such thrift**  
I have a strong feeling that I would be so successful

**That I should questionless be fortunate!**  
That I would undoubtedly win her!

---

**ANTONIO**  
**Thou know’st that all my fortunes are at sea;**  
You know all my money is invested in ships at sea;

**Neither have I money nor commodity**  
I don’t have ready cash or goods

**To raise a present sum.**  
To get a large amount right now.

**Therefore go forth:**  
So go ahead:

**Try what my credit can in Venice do;**  
See what my good credit in Venice can achieve;

**That shall be racked even to the uttermost**  
It will be stretched to the absolute limit

**To furnish thee to Belmont to fair Portia.**  
To equip you for your journey to Belmont and the beautiful Portia.

**Go presently inquire, and so will I,**  
Go ask around immediately, and so will I,

**Where money is, and I no question make**  
Where money can be borrowed, and I have no doubt

**To have it of my trust, or for my sake.**  
That I can get it on my reputation or for my sake.

**They exit.**
