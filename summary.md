PART I – REFERENCE TO CONTEXT (RTC)
EXTRACT 1
SHYLOCK:
"I hate him for he is a Christian,
But more for that in low simplicity
He lends out money gratis and brings down
The rate of usance here in Venice.
If I can catch him once upon the hip,
I will feed fat the ancient grudge I bear him."
________________________________________
(a) Who is speaking these lines? To whom are they addressed? Under what circumstances are they spoken?
Answer
These lines are spoken by Shylock, the Jewish moneylender, in an aside. They are not addressed to any character on stage but directly to the audience.
Shylock speaks these words after Antonio arrives to discuss the loan of three thousand ducats for Bassanio. While outwardly behaving politely, Shylock reveals his true feelings towards Antonio in this aside. The audience learns that Shylock's willingness to lend money is motivated not by generosity but by a desire for revenge.
________________________________________
(b) Why does Shylock hate Antonio? Give reasons.
Answer
Shylock hates Antonio for several reasons.
Firstly, Antonio is a Christian, and Shylock resents the prejudice and contempt that Christians show towards Jews.
Secondly, Antonio lends money without charging interest ("gratis"), thereby lowering the rate of usury in Venice and harming Shylock's business as a professional moneylender.
Thirdly, Antonio has repeatedly insulted and humiliated Shylock in public. He has called him a "dog", criticised his business practices, and even spat upon him.
Thus, Shylock's hatred is rooted in religious differences, economic rivalry, and personal humiliation.
________________________________________
(c) Explain the following phrases:
(i) "catch him once upon the hip"
This phrase comes from wrestling terminology and means to gain a decisive advantage over an opponent or catch someone in a vulnerable position from which escape is difficult.
Shylock means that if he ever gets an opportunity to trap Antonio legally, he will use it to his advantage.
________________________________________
(ii) "feed fat the ancient grudge"
This phrase means to satisfy, strengthen, and nurture a long-standing hatred.
The "ancient grudge" refers to Shylock's deep resentment against Antonio, which has developed over many years because of repeated insults and humiliation.
________________________________________
(d) What does this extract reveal about Shylock's character?
Answer
The extract reveals several important aspects of Shylock's character.
He is clearly revengeful, as he openly admits his desire to settle old scores with Antonio. He is also calculating and patient, waiting for the perfect opportunity to strike.
The extract further shows him to be deeply bitter and resentful, especially because of the discrimination and insults he has suffered as a Jew.
At the same time, Shakespeare presents Shylock as a complex character. While his desire for revenge is disturbing, the audience also understands that his bitterness arises from years of humiliation and prejudice.
________________________________________
Deeper Analysis
This extract is crucial because it reveals Shylock's true motive behind the proposed bond. The audience understands that the loan is not merely a business transaction but part of a carefully planned scheme for revenge.
The phrase "ancient grudge" introduces one of the central themes of the play—revenge versus mercy. It also creates dramatic irony because Antonio remains unaware of Shylock's real intentions.

ANTONIO:
"The devil can cite Scripture for his purpose!
An evil soul producing holy witness

________________________________________
(a) When does Antonio speak these lines?
Answer
Antonio speaks these lines after Shylock attempts to justify charging interest on loans by referring to the Biblical story of Jacob and Laban.
Shylock argues that Jacob prospered through cleverness and divine blessing. Antonio rejects this argument and accuses Shylock of misusing Scripture to defend greed and usury.
________________________________________
(b) Identify and explain the literary devices used in these lines.
Answer
Several literary devices are used:
Simile
"Like a villain with a smiling cheek"
Shylock is compared to a villain who appears pleasant outwardly but is evil within.
Metaphor
"A goodly apple rotten at the heart"
The rotten apple symbolizes a person whose outward appearance is attractive but whose inner nature is corrupt.
Biblical Allusion
The reference to Scripture and Biblical examples highlights the religious argument between Antonio and Shylock.
Imagery
The image of a beautiful apple concealing decay creates a vivid picture of hypocrisy and deception.

(c) What do these lines reveal about Antonio's attitude towards Shylock?
Answer
These lines reveal Antonio's strong distrust and dislike of Shylock.
Antonio believes that Shylock is dishonest and manipulative. He accuses him of twisting religious teachings for personal profit and views him as morally corrupt.
The lines also reveal Antonio's prejudice against Shylock. Even though he needs Shylock's financial help, he refuses to respect him and continues to view him with suspicion.
________________________________________
(d) Explain the dramatic irony in these lines.
Answer
The dramatic irony lies in the fact that Antonio correctly recognizes Shylock's hidden malice and compares him to a rotten apple concealed beneath a pleasant appearance.
However, despite understanding Shylock's nature, Antonio still agrees to sign the dangerous bond. The audience already knows from Shylock's aside that he intends to take revenge, making Antonio's confidence appear foolish and ironic.
________________________________________
Deeper Analysis
These lines highlight the important theme of appearance versus reality. Shylock appears friendly and generous, but his real intentions are far more dangerous.
The passage also exposes the deep religious conflict between Christians and Jews, which drives much of the action in the play.

EXTRACT 3
SHYLOCK:
"This is kind I offer."
BASSANIO:
"This were kindness!"
SHYLOCK:
"Go with me to a notary, seal me there
Your single bond; and, in a merry sport,
If you repay me not on such a day...
let the forfeit
Be nominated for an equal pound
Of your fair flesh..."
________________________________________
(a) Why does Shylock describe the bond as a "merry sport"?
Answer
Shylock describes the bond as a "merry sport" to make the condition appear harmless and amusing.
He wants Antonio and Bassanio to believe that the pound of flesh clause is merely a joke and will never actually be enforced. However, the audience knows that Shylock secretly hopes to use the bond as a means of taking revenge.
Thus, the phrase reflects Shylock's deceptive and cunning nature.
________________________________________
(b) Why does Bassanio object to the bond?
Answer
Bassanio immediately senses danger in Shylock's proposal.
Although Shylock presents the bond as an act of friendship, Bassanio distrusts him and feels uncomfortable with the unusual penalty. He fears that the agreement may have serious consequences and therefore urges Antonio not to accept it.
His caution contrasts sharply with Antonio's confidence.
________________________________________
(c) Why does Antonio readily agree to the bond?
Answer
Antonio agrees because he is completely confident that his ships will return safely before the bond expires.
Since he expects to receive far more money than the amount borrowed, he believes there is no possibility of forfeiting the bond. Antonio therefore dismisses Bassanio's fears and considers the agreement harmless.
This confidence later proves to be his tragic flaw.
________________________________________
(d) How does this extract contribute to the development of the plot?
Answer
This extract is one of the most important moments in the play because it introduces the famous pound-of-flesh bond, which becomes the central conflict of the story.
The bond links Antonio's future directly to Shylock's desire for revenge. It creates suspense, foreshadows the trial scene, and establishes the themes of justice, mercy, revenge, and fate.
The entire dramatic tension of the play grows from this agreement.
 
PART II – SHORT ANSWER QUESTIONS (ICSE MODEL ANSWERS)
The Merchant of Venice – Act 1, Scene 3
These answers are written in the style expected for 2–4 mark ICSE questions, combining textual knowledge, analysis, and thematic understanding.
________________________________________
Q1. Why does Shylock call the bond a “merry bond”?
Answer
Shylock calls the bond a "merry bond" to make it appear harmless and amusing. He pretends that the condition of taking a pound of Antonio's flesh is merely a joke and will never actually be enforced. However, the audience knows that Shylock secretly hates Antonio and sees the bond as an opportunity to satisfy his long-standing desire for revenge. Thus, the term "merry bond" is an example of dramatic irony because Shylock's true intentions are far from merry.
________________________________________
Q2. How does Shylock justify charging interest on loans?
Answer
Shylock justifies charging interest by referring to the Biblical story of Jacob and Laban. He explains how Jacob prospered by acquiring the streaked and spotted lambs from Laban's flock. Shylock argues that Jacob's success came through intelligence and God's blessing rather than dishonesty. Using this example, he claims that money, like livestock, can increase naturally and that earning profit through moneylending is therefore not sinful. Antonio, however, rejects this argument and accuses Shylock of misusing Scripture for personal gain.
________________________________________
Q3. Why does Bassanio object to the bond while Antonio agrees to it?
Answer
Bassanio objects to the bond because he instinctively distrusts Shylock and senses danger in the unusual condition attached to the loan. The idea of forfeiting a pound of flesh appears unnatural and alarming to him. Antonio, however, is extremely confident that his ships will return safely before the bond expires. Since he expects to receive several times the value of the loan, he believes there is no real risk involved and therefore agrees to the bond without hesitation.
________________________________________
Q4. What does Shylock mean when he says, “Suff’rance is the badge of all our tribe”?
Answer
By this statement, Shylock means that patience and endurance have become characteristic qualities of the Jewish people because they have suffered discrimination, insults, and persecution for many years. He suggests that Jews are accustomed to silently bearing humiliation from Christians. The line also helps the audience understand Shylock's sense of grievance and evokes sympathy for the difficulties faced by his community.
________________________________________
Q5. What risks threaten Antonio’s business ventures?
Answer
Antonio's wealth is invested in merchant ships travelling to various parts of the world. These ships face numerous dangers, including storms, shipwrecks, rocks, pirates, thieves, and unpredictable weather conditions. Shylock points out that ships are merely wooden vessels and sailors are ordinary men who can easily fall victim to misfortune. These risks make Antonio's financial position less secure than it appears.
________________________________________
Q6. What does Antonio’s behaviour reveal about his personality?
Answer
Antonio appears generous, loyal, and self-sacrificing because he is willing to risk his own financial security to help Bassanio. He is also courageous and straightforward, openly expressing his dislike for Shylock rather than pretending friendship. However, Antonio is proud and overconfident. His complete faith in the safe return of his ships prevents him from recognizing the danger hidden within Shylock's bond.
________________________________________
Q7. Why does Shylock refuse Bassanio’s invitation to dine with them?
Answer
Shylock refuses the invitation because Jewish religious customs forbade the eating of pork, which Christians commonly consumed. He expresses disgust at the idea of eating food associated with Christian practices. Although he is willing to conduct business with Christians, he refuses to socialize, dine, or pray with them. This highlights the religious divisions that exist between Jews and Christians in Venice.
________________________________________
Q8. Why does Shylock repeatedly mention “three thousand ducats”?
Answer
Shylock repeatedly mentions the amount of the loan because he is carefully calculating the financial details of the transaction. The repetition emphasizes his cautious and business-minded nature. It also creates suspense by showing that he is thinking deeply about the proposal and perhaps considering how he can use it to his advantage.
________________________________________
Q9. Why does Antonio dislike usury?
Answer
Antonio dislikes usury because Christian teachings traditionally condemned the practice of charging interest on loans. He believes that lending money should be an act of generosity rather than a means of making profit. For this reason, he lends money without interest and openly criticizes Shylock's profession as a moneylender.
________________________________________
Q10. What does Shylock mean by “Antonio is a good man”?
Answer
When Shylock says that Antonio is a "good man," he does not mean that Antonio is morally good. Instead, he means that Antonio is financially reliable and appears capable of repaying the loan. However, Shylock immediately points out that Antonio's wealth is tied up in risky overseas ventures, suggesting that his financial security is not as certain as it seems.
________________________________________
Q11. Why does Shylock say, “I will buy with you, sell with you, talk with you, walk with you, and so following; but I will not eat with you, drink with you, nor pray with you”?
Answer
Shylock means that he is willing to engage in business dealings with Christians because such interactions are necessary for commerce. However, he refuses to share meals, drinks, or religious practices with them because of deep religious differences and mutual distrust. This statement highlights the social and religious separation between Jews and Christians in Venetian society.
________________________________________
Q12. What does Antonio mean when he says, “The devil can cite Scripture for his purpose”?
Answer
Antonio means that even evil people can quote religious texts to support their selfish or dishonest motives. He accuses Shylock of using the Bible selectively to justify charging interest on loans. Through this statement, Antonio suggests that religious arguments can be manipulated to serve personal interests rather than moral truth.
________________________________________
Q13. Why is the pound of flesh bond significant?
Answer
The pound of flesh bond is significant because it becomes the central conflict of the play. It transforms an ordinary financial agreement into a life-threatening contract. The bond reflects Shylock's desire for revenge, Antonio's overconfidence, and the themes of justice, mercy, and fate. The later courtroom scene revolves entirely around this agreement.
________________________________________
Q14. How does Shakespeare create suspense in this scene?
Answer
Shakespeare creates suspense by allowing the audience to hear Shylock's private thoughts through his asides. The audience learns about his hatred for Antonio and his desire for revenge, while Antonio remains unaware of the danger. The unusual condition of the bond and Bassanio's repeated warnings further increase the tension and foreshadow future trouble.
________________________________________
Q15. Why is Act 1, Scene 3 considered a turning point in the play?
Answer
Act 1, Scene 3 is considered a turning point because it introduces the pound-of-flesh bond that drives the entire plot forward. The scene reveals Shylock's true motives, establishes the conflict between justice and mercy, and creates dramatic tension that eventually leads to the famous trial scene. Without this bond, the central conflict of the play would not exist.

These are ICSE Board-level model answers, written in a structured format with introduction, development, analysis, and conclusion.

Q1. Describe Shylock's attitude towards Antonio in Act 1, Scene 3.
Answer
In Act 1, Scene 3, Shylock's attitude towards Antonio is dominated by hatred, resentment, and a desire for revenge. Throughout the scene, Shakespeare reveals that Shylock's feelings arise from a combination of religious prejudice, economic rivalry, and personal humiliation.
Firstly, Shylock openly admits that he hates Antonio because he is a Christian. The strained relationship between Christians and Jews forms an important background to the play. Shylock feels that Christians look down upon Jews and treat them unfairly.
Secondly, Antonio harms Shylock economically. As a moneylender, Shylock earns his living by charging interest on loans. Antonio, however, lends money without interest and thereby reduces the rate of usury in Venice. Shylock therefore views Antonio as a threat to his livelihood.
Thirdly, Antonio has repeatedly insulted and humiliated him in public. Shylock recalls how Antonio has called him a "misbeliever" and a "cut-throat dog," spat upon his Jewish clothing, and openly criticized his business practices. These insults have deeply wounded Shylock's pride and dignity.
Although Shylock behaves politely during the negotiation, his aside reveals his true intentions. He hopes to "catch him once upon the hip" and "feed fat the ancient grudge" he bears against Antonio. The proposed bond is therefore not simply a financial agreement but a carefully planned instrument of revenge.
However, Shakespeare does not portray Shylock as a simple villain. His bitterness is partly the result of the prejudice and humiliation he has suffered. The audience may therefore understand the reasons behind his hatred even while condemning his desire for revenge.
In conclusion, Shylock's attitude towards Antonio combines religious hostility, economic competition, wounded pride, and calculated vengeance. This hatred becomes the driving force behind the central conflict of the play.
________________________________________
Q2. Bring out the contrast between Antonio and Shylock as presented in Act 1, Scene 3.
Answer
Act 1, Scene 3 presents a striking contrast between Antonio and Shylock in terms of religion, values, personality, and attitude towards money.
The most obvious difference lies in their religious identities. Antonio is a Christian, while Shylock is a Jew. This religious divide creates mistrust and hostility between them from the beginning.
Their attitudes towards money are also completely different. Antonio lends money without charging interest because he believes that helping others should not be a means of profit. Shylock, on the other hand, regards moneylending as a legitimate business and charges interest on loans. This difference becomes a major source of conflict.
In terms of personality, Antonio appears generous, noble, and loyal. He is willing to risk his own safety to help Bassanio obtain the money he needs. Shylock, however, appears cautious, calculating, and business-minded. He carefully examines Antonio's financial position before agreeing to the loan.
The two men also differ in the way they express themselves. Antonio speaks openly and directly, even admitting that he may insult Shylock again. Shylock, however, conceals his true feelings beneath polite and courteous language. His aside reveals the hatred that he keeps hidden from others.
Despite these differences, Shakespeare does not present either character as entirely good or entirely evil. Antonio is generous but prejudiced. Shylock is revengeful but has also suffered discrimination and humiliation.
Thus, Act 1, Scene 3 presents Antonio and Shylock as opposing forces whose conflict drives the action of the play.
________________________________________
Q3. How does Act 1, Scene 3 prepare the ground for the future conflict in the play?
Answer
Act 1, Scene 3 is one of the most important scenes in The Merchant of Venice because it lays the foundation for the central conflict of the play.
The most significant event in the scene is the creation of the pound-of-flesh bond. Shylock agrees to lend Bassanio three thousand ducats on Antonio's security, but adds the unusual condition that he may claim a pound of Antonio's flesh if the loan is not repaid on time. This bond later becomes the basis of the famous trial scene.
The scene also establishes Shylock's desire for revenge. Through his aside, the audience learns that Shylock hates Antonio and hopes to use the bond as a means of satisfying his "ancient grudge." This creates dramatic irony because Antonio remains unaware of Shylock's true intentions.
Antonio's overconfidence further contributes to the future conflict. He believes that his ships will return safely before the bond expires and therefore sees no danger in accepting the condition. His confidence blinds him to the risk involved.
The scene also introduces important themes such as revenge versus mercy, prejudice, friendship, risk, and appearance versus reality. These themes continue to shape the events of the play.
In conclusion, Act 1, Scene 3 prepares the ground for future conflict by introducing the bond, revealing Shylock's motives, and establishing the circumstances that eventually lead to Antonio's crisis and the courtroom drama.
________________________________________
Q4. Examine the theme of revenge in Act 1, Scene 3.
Answer
The theme of revenge dominates Act 1, Scene 3 and becomes the driving force behind the action of the play.
Shylock's desire for revenge is clearly revealed in his aside when he declares that he hates Antonio and wishes to "feed fat the ancient grudge" he bears against him. His hatred is the result of years of humiliation, insults, and discrimination.
Antonio has publicly mocked Shylock, criticized his profession, called him insulting names, and treated him with contempt. Shylock has endured these wrongs outwardly but has secretly nurtured his resentment.
The loan negotiation provides Shylock with an opportunity to take revenge legally. Instead of openly attacking Antonio, he cleverly disguises his plan beneath the appearance of friendship and generosity. The pound-of-flesh bond becomes a weapon through which he hopes to punish his enemy.
Shakespeare presents revenge as a destructive emotion. Shylock's obsession prevents him from acting with mercy and transforms a simple business agreement into a dangerous trap.
At the same time, Shakespeare encourages the audience to understand why Shylock seeks revenge. His desire for vengeance arises from genuine suffering and humiliation. Thus, the play presents revenge as both understandable and morally dangerous.

Q5. Discuss the theme of appearance versus reality in Act 1, Scene 3.
Answer
The theme of appearance versus reality plays an important role in Act 1, Scene 3.
Throughout the scene, Shylock presents himself as friendly and generous. He claims that he wishes to forget past quarrels, become Antonio's friend, and lend the money without charging interest. On the surface, his behaviour appears kind and reasonable.
In reality, however, Shylock is motivated by hatred and revenge. His aside reveals his true feelings long before Antonio becomes aware of them. The audience therefore knows that his offer of friendship is not genuine.
The pound-of-flesh bond itself reflects this theme. Shylock describes it as a "merry bond" and a "merry sport," suggesting that it is merely a joke. Yet beneath this harmless appearance lies a deadly condition that could cost Antonio his life.
Antonio also recognizes this contrast when he remarks, "The devil can cite Scripture for his purpose" and compares Shylock to "a goodly apple rotten at the heart." These images suggest that outward appearances can conceal inner corruption.
Thus, Shakespeare uses this scene to warn that people and situations may not always be what they seem. The contrast between appearance and reality becomes one of the most important themes in the play.
________________________________________
Q6. Show how Shakespeare creates dramatic irony in Act 1, Scene 3.
Answer
Dramatic irony occurs when the audience knows something that the characters do not. Shakespeare uses this technique effectively throughout Act 1, Scene 3.
The clearest example occurs in Shylock's aside. The audience learns that Shylock hates Antonio and wishes to take revenge on him. However, Antonio and Bassanio are unaware of these feelings.
Another example occurs when Shylock describes the bond as a "merry sport." The audience understands that he does not genuinely regard it as a joke. Instead, he sees it as a means of carrying out his revenge. Antonio, however, accepts Shylock's explanation without suspicion.
Antonio's confidence also contributes to the dramatic irony. He believes that his ships will return safely before the bond expires and therefore considers the agreement harmless. The audience, however, senses that his confidence may be misplaced.
This dramatic irony creates suspense because the audience anticipates the danger that Antonio fails to recognize. It also increases the emotional impact of later events when the bond becomes a serious threat.
Therefore, dramatic irony plays a crucial role in building tension and preparing the audience for the conflict that follows.
Character Sketch of Shylock
Question
Sketch the character of Shylock as revealed in Act 1, Scene 3.
Answer
In Act 1, Scene 3, Shakespeare presents Shylock as one of the most complex and memorable characters in the play. He appears both as a victim of prejudice and as a man consumed by revenge.
The most striking aspect of Shylock's character is his deep hatred for Antonio. Through his aside, he openly admits that he hates Antonio because he is a Christian and because Antonio lends money without charging interest. He also resents the public insults and humiliation he has suffered at Antonio's hands. This hatred motivates his desire to "feed fat the ancient grudge" he bears against Antonio.
Shylock is also revengeful and vindictive. Instead of confronting Antonio openly, he patiently waits for an opportunity to trap him. The proposed pound-of-flesh bond reveals how carefully he plans his revenge. Although he presents the bond as a "merry sport," his real intention is far more sinister.
Another important quality is his intelligence and cunning. He is a skilled businessman who carefully evaluates Antonio's financial position before agreeing to the loan. He cleverly uses Biblical references, such as the story of Jacob and Laban, to defend the practice of charging interest. His ability to hide his true motives beneath polite language demonstrates his shrewdness.
At the same time, Shylock is a victim of prejudice and discrimination. He reminds Antonio of the insults, spitting, and abuse he has endured because he is a Jew. His statement, "Suff'rance is the badge of all our tribe," highlights the suffering of the Jewish community and evokes sympathy from the audience.
Shylock is also proud of his identity and religion. He refuses to dine with Christians and maintains a clear distinction between his faith and theirs. This pride contributes to the conflict between him and Antonio.
However, his desire for revenge ultimately overshadows his positive qualities. Instead of rising above the injustice he has suffered, he allows bitterness to control his actions.
In conclusion, Shylock emerges as a complex character who is intelligent, determined, and deeply wounded by prejudice. Shakespeare presents him neither as a pure villain nor as a complete victim but as a tragic figure whose hatred drives the central conflict of the play.

Character Sketch of Antonio
Question
Give a character sketch of Antonio as presented in Act 1, Scene 3.
Answer
Antonio is presented as a generous, loyal, and honourable merchant whose strengths are balanced by significant flaws.
His most admirable quality is his friendship and generosity. He is willing to risk his own financial security in order to help Bassanio obtain the money he needs. Without hesitation, he agrees to become the guarantor for the loan, demonstrating his deep affection and loyalty towards his friend.
Antonio is also courageous and straightforward. Unlike many people, he does not hide his feelings. Even when he needs Shylock's help, he openly admits that he dislikes him and may continue to insult him in the future. His honesty and fearlessness make him a dignified character.
Another important trait is his confidence. Antonio firmly believes that his ships will return safely before the bond expires. This confidence enables him to accept the bond without fear.
However, this confidence becomes overconfidence, which may be regarded as his tragic flaw. He completely ignores Bassanio's warnings and underestimates the danger hidden in Shylock's proposal. His inability to foresee the consequences of the bond places him in serious danger.
Antonio also displays religious prejudice. Although he is often viewed as a noble Christian gentleman, he openly insults Shylock because he is a Jew and condemns his profession as a moneylender. His treatment of Shylock reveals a less admirable side of his character.
Furthermore, Antonio values friendship above personal gain. He is willing to place himself at risk for Bassanio's happiness and never hesitates when his friend requires assistance.
In conclusion, Antonio is a noble and generous character whose loyalty and courage make him admirable. However, his prejudice and excessive confidence contribute to the difficulties he later faces.

Character Sketch of Bassanio
Question
Describe Bassanio's character as revealed in Act 1, Scene 3.
Answer
Although Bassanio plays a smaller role in this scene than Antonio and Shylock, Shakespeare reveals several important aspects of his character.
Bassanio is shown to be a loving and loyal friend. He approaches Shylock only because he needs money to pursue his plans of marrying Portia. However, he remains concerned about Antonio's safety throughout the negotiation.
One of Bassanio's most important qualities is his good judgment. Unlike Antonio, he immediately senses that there is something dangerous about the proposed bond. When Shylock suggests the pound-of-flesh penalty, Bassanio objects strongly and urges Antonio not to accept it. His reaction demonstrates caution and practical wisdom.
Bassanio is also respectful and courteous. Throughout the conversation, he treats Shylock politely despite the tension between Shylock and Antonio. His diplomatic behaviour contrasts with Antonio's blunt hostility.
At the same time, Bassanio is dependent upon others financially. His need for money has led him into debt, and he relies on Antonio's generosity to support his ambitions. This dependence drives the action of the scene and indirectly leads to the dangerous agreement.
Another admirable quality is his concern for Antonio's welfare. Even though the loan is intended to help him, he is willing to abandon the plan rather than allow Antonio to accept such a risky condition. This shows genuine affection and gratitude towards his friend.
In conclusion, Bassanio appears as a loyal, thoughtful, and sensible young man. While he depends on Antonio financially, he demonstrates maturity and caution by recognizing the danger of Shylock's proposal before anyone else does.

QUOTATION 1
"I hate him for he is a Christian."
Speaker
Shylock
Context
Shylock speaks these words in an aside after Antonio enters during the loan negotiations. While outwardly appearing polite, he privately reveals his true feelings to the audience.
Meaning
Shylock openly admits his hatred for Antonio because he is a Christian. The statement reveals the deep religious hostility that exists between Jews and Christians in Venice.
Significance
•	Reveals Shylock's prejudice.
•	Shows the religious tensions of Venetian society.
•	Introduces the theme of revenge.
•	Explains one of the reasons behind Shylock's actions.
Themes
•	Religious Conflict
•	Prejudice
•	Revenge
Literary Device
Aside
The audience hears Shylock's true thoughts while the other characters remain unaware.
Examination Tip
Use this quotation whenever discussing:
•	Shylock's hatred
•	Religious prejudice
•	Causes of the conflict
________________________________________
QUOTATION 2
"He lends out money gratis and brings down the rate of usance."
Speaker
Shylock
Context
Shylock continues explaining why he hates Antonio.
Meaning
Antonio lends money without charging interest ("gratis"), thereby lowering the rate of interest in Venice and damaging Shylock's moneylending business.
Significance
This quotation reveals that Shylock's hatred is not purely religious. It also arises from economic rivalry.
Themes
•	Usury versus Charity
•	Business Competition
•	Revenge
Character Insight
Shows Shylock as:
•	Practical
•	Business-minded
•	Concerned about profit
Examination Tip
Use this quotation to explain:
•	Why Shylock hates Antonio
•	The economic conflict in the play
________________________________________
QUOTATION 3
"If I can catch him once upon the hip, I will feed fat the ancient grudge I bear him."
Speaker
Shylock
Context
Shylock reveals his secret desire to take revenge on Antonio.
Meaning
The phrase "catch him once upon the hip" means gaining a decisive advantage over an opponent. Shylock hopes to trap Antonio legally and satisfy his long-standing hatred.
Significance
This is one of the strongest expressions of revenge in the play.
Themes
•	Revenge
•	Hatred
•	Justice versus Mercy
Literary Device
Metaphorical Wrestling Imagery
The phrase comes from wrestling terminology.
Character Insight
Shows Shylock as:
•	Vindictive
•	Patient
•	Calculating
Examination Tip
This quotation is ideal for:
•	Character sketches of Shylock
•	Theme of revenge
•	RTC answers
